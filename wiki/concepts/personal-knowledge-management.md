# Personal Knowledge Management (PKM)

Personal Knowledge Management refers to the process of capturing, organizing, and synthesizing information to enhance learning and creativity. The industry has shifted from linear note-taking to networked thought.

## Modern Paradigms

### Graph-Based Management
Represented by [[obsidian-2026-obsidian]], this approach emphasizes "bi-directional linking." Instead of a rigid folder hierarchy, knowledge is viewed as a web.
- **Strengths**: Emergent structure, serendipitous discovery, mimics human associative memory.
- **Weaknesses**: Can become a "messy" graph if not curated; higher friction in initial organization.

### Outline-Based Management
Represented by [[logseq-2026-logseq]], this approach uses a "block-based" atomic structure (outliners).
- **Strengths**: High granularity, easier for rapid capture, structural flexibility via indents.
- **Weaknesses**: Less intuitive for long-form writing compared to traditional documents.

## Synthesis: Graph vs Outline
While Obsidian focuses on the *document* as the unit of thought, Logseq focuses on the *bullet/block*. Both converge on the use of a "Knowledge Graph" to visualize connections, but they differ in how they treat the primary interface: one is a digital notebook, the other is a structured journal.

## Bias Checks
- **Counter-arguments**: Some argue that "digital gardens" and complex PKM systems create "collector's fallacy," where the act of organizing information is mistaken for the act of learning.
- **Data gaps**: Lack of long-term longitudinal studies on whether networked note-taking actually improves cognitive recall compared to traditional methods.
