# Lessons from starting, building, and exiting a devtools startup

Author: Boris Tane
Date: Jun 7, 2024

## Key Learnings

### Be obsessed with finding Product Market Fit (PMF)
Your one and only mission as an early-stage founder is to find PMF as quickly as possible. Nothing else matters. Avoid over-focusing on software architecture, clean code, tests, and scalability too early. Iterate fast, measure results, and discard what doesn't work.

### Better, faster, cheaper is not enough anymore
In the current developer tools space, simply being better, faster, or cheaper is insufficient because incumbents iterate quickly. Look for technological shifts and find areas where you can be a monopoly.

### Don’t be stubborn
If something is not growing fast, drop it quickly. Avoid sticking to a thesis (like "Focus on a single problem") if market signals indicate a shift is necessary.

### Do things that don’t scale, really
Don't rely solely on automation for early growth. Tedious, manual work (like personally messaging potential users on Twitter) can be the most effective way to get initial signups and sales.

### Create hype, don’t be shy
Technical founders often avoid "hype," but it is a crucial tool for public awareness. Ensure developers know your solution exists, even if they don't sign up immediately.

### Get a side project
Building a startup is demanding; maintain a hobby project where you can build random, useless things without pressure to solve a problem. This preserves your happiness and love for building software.

### Exiting a company is hard
Selling a company involves significant legal and accounting work and can be emotionally taxing as the company is often a part of the founder's identity.
